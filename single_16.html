by AKSHAYA AJITH

Nowadays, technological progress is developing with unseen speed and power. It may even look like everyday something new is created and its potential is exhaustless. Technological advance has captured all the people on the planet in both positive and negative meaning of this word.

Space adventure by humans was one of a milestone. A new problem really haunts space explorations and that is space debris.

What is space debris?

Space junk or space trash is a term for defunct human-made objects in space, principally in earth’s orbit. These include derelict spacecrafts, abandoned launch vehicle stages. Even paint flecks, solidified liquids expelled from spacecraft , and unburned particles from solid rocket motors add to it . This represents a risk to the spacecraft.

In order to tackle this problem of increasing space junks, scientists from the KYOTO university in Japan have teamed up with a company specializing in construction using wooden materials to build the world's first wooden satellites by 2023.

The researchers believe that wood can simply burn up easily without propelling the debris back to Earth. It does not release any harmful substance into the atmosphere. Sumitomo Forestry, part of the Sumitomo Group, which was founded more than 40 years ago, said it would work on developing wooden materials highly resistant to temperature changes and sunlight. The word it is using is an “Research and Development secret“.

“TECHNOLOGY LIKE ART IS A SOARING EXERCISE OF THE HUMAN IMAGINATION ” — Daniel Bell

Takao Doi, Japanese astronaut, engineer and veteran of two NASA space shuttles missions is part of this team. He said “The next stage will be developing the engineering model of the satellite, then we will manufacture the flight model”.

Wooden satellites appear to be the latest in a series of endeavours to avoid the accumulation of junk in space, which had led researchers to explore options like sending “space-whips” to knock space debris out of orbit, employing giant magnets, harpoons or nets to bring debris down.

According to a theory proposed by former NASA scientist Donald Kessler in 1978, excessive space junk can initiate a reaction causing more and more objects to collide, to the point where Earth’s orbit itself becomes unusable for satellites. This could in turn impact humankind as we and our modern life aspects rely on satellites, GPS and so on. As such, wooden satellites are a new innovative insight to this hazard.
